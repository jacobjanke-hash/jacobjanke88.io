Project Overview

Every semester, thousands of college students struggle to reach their academic goals despite investing long hours of study. This project investigates whether it's just about how much you study — or whether how you study matters more.

Using data from 250 undergraduate students across five majors, we analyze the relationships between study habits, lifestyle factors, and GPA. We employ both traditional statistical modeling (multiple linear regression) and modern machine learning (random forest) to identify the strongest predictors of academic success.

Research Questions: (1) Which study habits and lifestyle factors most strongly predict GPA? (2) Can we build a reliable machine learning model to predict academic performance from these factors?

The Data

The dataset contains self-reported survey responses from 250 undergraduate students at a mid-sized university, collected during the Fall semester. Variables capture study behavior, sleep patterns, class attendance, stress, and academic performance.

Analytical Approach

We used a two-pronged analytical strategy to explore and model the relationships in the data:

📊

Exploratory Data Analysis

Distribution analysis, scatter plots with trend lines, correlation heatmaps, and grouped comparisons across majors and class years to identify patterns.

📈

Multiple Linear Regression

Traditional parametric model predicting GPA from study hours, sleep, active recall, attendance, and stress. Validated with ANOVA and diagnostic plots.

🌲

Random Forest (ML)

Ensemble machine learning model with 500 trees trained on a 75/25 train-test split. Provides variable importance rankings and non-linear modeling.

🔮

Model Comparison

Head-to-head evaluation of both models on held-out test data using RMSE, R², and MAE metrics to assess predictive accuracy.

Key Findings

  • Study hours matter, but quality matters more. Students using active recall techniques achieve notably higher GPAs at every level of study investment.
  • Sleep is not optional. Sleep hours showed a significant positive association with GPA. Students getting fewer than 6 hours consistently underperformed.
  • Show up. Attendance rate was a robust predictor across all models — simply being present provides a measurable academic benefit.
  • Stress is a silent GPA killer. Higher self-reported stress was significantly associated with lower GPAs, independent of study habits.
  • Both models agree. Linear regression and random forest identified the same top predictors, providing strong converging evidence.

Model Performance: Both models explain a substantial share of GPA variance. The linear regression and random forest achieved comparable R² values on held-out test data, suggesting the underlying relationships are largely linear in this dataset.
